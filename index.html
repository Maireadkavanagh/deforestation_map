Prehistory

In the early Holocene, before the arrival of humans, Ireland was dominated by broadleaved forest cover. This forest cover appeared after the retreat of ice following the last ice age.

Appearance of Humans

Humans appeared in Ireland in the mid-Holocene. They began to clear small areas of forest cover to make space for agriculture and settlement building. This also coincides in a sharp decrease in the total cover of Elm in the forest canopy, sparking a theory that humans acted as a vector for disease from mainland Europe.

Cromwellian Era

Deforestation increased with the further strengthening of British control in Ireland, with the building of plantation towns requiring a greater supply of wood. This trend continued for a further 300 years, as an increasing population resulted in more land being cleared and used in agriculture.

Post 1950's - Present Day

By the end of the twentieth century, the total forest cover in Ireland had been reduced to around 1%. Some further deforestation occurred in the 1940's to help with supplies during the emergency. Beginning in the 1950's, the planting of trees as a commercial crop began to take hold. Through extensive planting of fast-growing conifers, the total forest cover in Ireland now stands at around 10%.
